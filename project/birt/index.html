Development of a biodiversity index for residential towns

Jun 2016

The rapid growth of urban areas worldwide exposes wildlife to new man-made stresses that can drastically change the composition of animal communities. Measuring and understanding the impacts of the urban environment on biodiversity are becoming increasingly important.

Environmental indices and metrics can be useful tools to evaluate the effectiveness of actions aimed at promoting conservation or mitigating the loss of biodiversity. Environmental impact assessments are commonly used to measure the impact of new urban developments, and serve to protect biodiversity through legislation. However, these assessments often rely on records of species occurrence collected opportunistically (i.e. without a standardised sampling strategy), at spatial scales that tend to be too coarse for use in urban design and planning.

This project aims to develop a probabilistic index of biodiversity, based on an understanding of how landscape components impact four animal groups—birds, butterflies, odonates and amphibians. Remotely sensed (e.g. satellite imagery, LiDAR) and open data sources are used to streamline data collection and generalise findings across the wider landscape. The efficacy of various national policy tools (e.g. Green Plot Ratio) in enriching wildlife are also assessed.

The project is funded from 2016–2022 by the Singapore Housing Development Board ($1,286,980), awarded to the National University of Singapore. The lead principal investigator is Dr. Chong Kwek Yan.
