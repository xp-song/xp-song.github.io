The natural capital of city trees

Jan 2017 2 min read

Over the past decades, there has been intensive effort to ‘green’ many cities around the world. Increasing the amount of tree cover has been particularly important, as it is associated with numerous benefits through the ‘ecosystem services’ they provide to city dwellers. For example, trees can lower temperatures by providing shade, mitigate air pollution by adsorbing pollutants, and reduce the risk of flooding by intercepting rainfall. Trees are thus a form of ‘natural capital’ that produce value for people.

Many cities have started to assess the status and health of these natural capital assets, which is an important step to incorporate the environment into holistic, national-scale planning. However, it is also crucial to weigh these benefits against the costs of tree planting and maintenance. There is also a need to consider the effect of time—specifically, how the growth and mortality of trees affect their associated benefits and costs as they age. A better understanding of these relationships will help us maximise the cost-effectiveness of trees in the long run.
