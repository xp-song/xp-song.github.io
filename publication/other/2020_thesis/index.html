Exploring big data applications for urban greening

Xiao Ping Song
June 2020
Project Video Source Document

Abstract

Research on urban greening is inherently multi-disciplinary. Plants and green spaces in cities are part of a complex interplay of environmental and social factors, and have the potential to moderate the negative impacts of urbanisation. In recent years, vast quantities of electronic data—often referred to as ‘big data’—offer new opportunities to extract insights about our environment and human activities. This has generated much enthusiasm in the industry and research community, but the application of such methods to urban greening has been relatively slow. This thesis explores the use of novel data and techniques for understanding the social, economic and ecological impacts of urban greening. Two main topics are examined: the performance of urban trees using data from tree inventories; and preferences for outdoor recreation based on the count and content of social media photographs. The potential and shortcomings of applying such techniques to urban greening practice are discussed.
